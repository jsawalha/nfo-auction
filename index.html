Passivity loses

Low conversion is good. It means you bid on players you don't win — draining rivals, learning their price points, staying in every conversation. The most passive GM finishes last; the most aggressive price-enforcer finishes 4th.

ReD PantY NiTe bids on just 29 players and wins 48% of them — the most passive GM in the league. No market leverage; they pay full retail on everything. Down by the Rivers bids on 56 players and wins only 25% — the premier price-enforcer. Target a conversion rate of 25–35%, not 48%.

3. The tier-squeeze myth

The common belief: the last 1–2 players in a tier get bid up as supply runs out. In this league, the data says the opposite.

PositionFirst taken in tierLast taken in tierSpread
RB+$1.3−$2.3last is $3.6 cheaper
WR+$4.3−$0.6last is $4.9 cheaper
Demand is front-loaded, not back-loaded. Only 22% of tiers had the last player go above the tier's earlier average. By the time a tier is running out, the managers who wanted that tier already got their guy and moved on — so the last one sells at a discount. The "squeeze" is really the opener's premium in disguise.

The play

  • Never open a position's bidding — the first buyer pays the tax (up to $6.5 on WR). Let a rival nominate the first WR/TE.
  • Be the last buyer in a tier, not the first — last-in-tier runs ~$3–5 cheaper as demand thins.
  • But respect the cliff. Don't wait so long that the tier empties and you fall off — especially RB round 3→4, a $17 drop. Patience within a tier is bounded by the cliff below it.
  • Fade the mid-RB run. When RBs start flying, the 6th–9th go +$2.2 hot — that's the moment to pivot to WR value or wait for the next RB tier.

2. Watch the speed

After controlling for value, bid intensity predicts overpayment (total bids is the strongest signal, +0.41). Among contested players:

War typeOver / under modelProfile

They all say the same thing: the amount of bidding is the tell. Whether a war drags on or the bids fly rapid-fire, lots of bidding = someone's overpaying. A player grabbed with few bids that resolve fast comes in below value.

The play

  • Sparse & quick (2–4 bidders, bidding peters out) → your window — pounce. You'll win at or under model.
  • Crowded & grinding (5th bidder jumps in, bids keep coming past ~10) → fade it. That player is going ~$3+ over value; if he's not a core target, let someone else eat the overpay.
  • The 5th bidder is the trigger. Below it you're fine; at it, the premium kicks in.
Honest caveat: a crowded, grinding war may mean the room correctly re-rated a player after ADP was set (a camp riser, a role change) — info the model can't see. So use the paddle-count as a live signal to reassess, not blind gospel: if five sharp GMs are all-in on a guy your sheet has cheap, your number might be stale. If it's a random pile-on, hold your max and walk.

3. What to nominate, and when

  • Early → nominate expensive players you do NOT want. Double win: you drain rivals' budgets and they eat the full-wallet premium. (Exactly what San Di Lello and Down by the Rivers do.) The only thing to buy early is an anchor — you'll pay ~+$1.0, the unavoidable price of certainty, since 96% of $30+ studs sell in the first third.
  • Middle → still a premium (+$1.0). Don't spend here on fungible players. This is the dead zone that sinks the bottom teams.
  • Late → this is where your darts live. The residual finally flips negative (−$0.2 to −$1.2). Nominate and buy your cheap targets here.
  • Never nominate a cheap player you actually want, early. It's the worst option available: you pay the full-wallet premium and tip your hand on someone nobody was thinking about yet.
The rule that ties it together: wait on fungible players, never on scarce ones.
Fungible (dollar darts, deep WRs) — dozens of substitutes, and darts hit only ~15% anyway, so losing any specific one costs nothing. Wait. Across 5–6 darts that's ~$9 saved — a free WR upgrade.
Scarce (RB anchors, the last player above a tier cliff) — no substitute exists (RB waiver hit rate is just 22%). Pay the early premium and take him.

⚠ Only 24 cheap players were nominated early across 5 drafts, so that +$1.3 specifically is directional. The direction is solid — confirmed by the well-sampled middle (+$1.0, n=144) and late (−$0.2, n=214) buckets.

Using the Tier Watch live

The Tier Watch strip on the draft board answers one question: "if I pass on this guy, what's actually left behind him?" It's the antidote to the two ways this league loses money — panic-buying because a run scared you, and waiting patiently for a tier that already emptied.

⚠ The counts are not automatic. They update from the players you mark off the board, plus your own picks. If you stop marking sales, the strip silently freezes and starts lying to you. One click per sale — do it every time, especially in the first hour when 57% of the money moves.

3. How to actually use it

  • Read the band, not the run. We tested "pounce after a positional run" directly — when RB1–3 go back-to-back and a lower-ranked RB comes up, he sells for exactly his model price (residual −$0.0, n=46). Scarcity pushes up, budget-drain pushes down, and they cancel. Three RBs going in a row is meaningless; 2 left ≥$40 is a signal.
  • Amber band → pay up to your walk-away. The choice is no longer "this guy at $42 vs. someone similar at $36." It's "this guy at $42 vs. nothing like him." Go to your 75th-percentile walk-away — but not past it.
  • Amber cliff → get your guy now. Roughly three sales before the market re-prices $11 lower. Paying $40 above the cliff beats paying $29 below it, because the $29 player is a materially worse player — the price dropped for a reason.
  • CLIFF PASSED → go quiet and let it come to you. This is where your bench gets built. Nominate elsewhere, let others chase, take the $25–29 tier at $25–29.
  • Cross-reference before you commit. The Tier Watch says how urgent, not how expensive. Two live checks override it: count the paddles (5+ bidders ≈ $3 over model regardless of scarcity, +0.41 correlation — the strongest live signal we have), and check the market tracker (if the room is hot, every band costs more than the board says).
The failure mode it's built to prevent. ReD PantY NiTe's signature loss is dumping 41% of the budget in the dead zone — after the cliffs have passed, on players the strip would have shown as a picked-over tier. If it's an hour in, your RB and WR bands are red, and you're still holding $120, the Tier Watch has been telling you for a while. Believe it.
One-line rule: amber means act, red means move on, and a run means nothing.

GM scouting reports

Key takeaways

  1. Middle-third spending is the strongest predictor of failure. Last place spends 41% there; top-3 spend 22–25%. Get money in early or hold it for darts — never drift into the middle.
  2. Passive bidding loses. Bid on 40–55 players, win 14. Low conversion = leverage.
  3. The $10–25 RB trap costs the field $22–40 per team. Avoiding it is free money.
  4. Points-per-dollar and hit% predict finish — not positional allocation. RB-heavy teams finish 4th and 10th.
  5. Two valid QB paths: buy a cheap one at auction, or punt and stream. Paying up does not work.
  6. Inflation is localized, and the squeeze is a myth. Never open a position (the first WR pays +$6.5); be the last buyer in a tier (~$3–5 cheaper), but don't fall off the cliff below it.
  7. Count the paddles. ≤4 bidders = par or bargain; the 5th paddle means ~$3 over value. Sparse-and-quick wars are your window; crowded, grinding wars are overpays to fade.
  8. Wait on fungible players, never on scarce ones. Cheap targets cost ~$1.50 more early than late — nominate expensive guys you don't want early, and buy your darts once the room is broke.

Caveats. 3 seasons × 10 GMs = 30 franchise-seasons; individual GM averages rest on only 3 data points each — treat single-GM numbers as directional, not precise. "Finish" = regular-season record + points-for; playoffBracket was never scraped, so championships aren't measured. "Hit %" = the player returned startable production at his position that year.
